Charcoal

This page shows how the topic of "Charcoal" evolved across different Masonic documents throughout history. Each row represents a different document, showing the chronological development of this particular aspect of Masonic ritual and instruction.

Document Text Content
1730 Prichard Q. How long do you serve your Master?
A. From Monday Morning to Saturday Night.
Q. How do you serve him?
A. With Chalk, Charcoal and Earthen Pan.
Q. What do they denote?
A. Freedom, Fervency and Zeal.
1777 Wilson M. What do you work with?
W. With chalk and charcoal.
M. What do these words signify?
W. These signify liberty, fervour, and constancy.
1802 Browne Q: With what did you serve?
A: With freedom, fervency and zeal.
Q: They are excellent qualities; what are they composed of?
A: Chalk, charcoal and clay.
Q: Please to explain them!
A: There is nothing so Free for the Use of MAN as Chalk, as it seldom Touches, but it leaves its Trace.
There is nothing so Fervent as Charcoal, for when once well Lighted, no Metal is able to resist its Force.
There is nothing so Zealous as Clay, our Mother Earth, which is always labouring for our Welfare, from whence we Came, and to which we must Return, as that will receive us, when all our Friends have forsaken us.
1802 Finch Q: How did you serve?
A: With Freedom, Fervency, and Zeal.
Q: What are the emblems of Freedom, Fervency, and Zeal?
A: Chalk, Charcoal, and Clay.
Q: Why so?
A: Nothing is more free for the use of man than chalk, which seldom touches but leaves its trace behind; nothing is more fervent than charcoal, for when well lighted no metal is able to resist its force; nothing more zealous than clay, our mother earth, who will kindly open her arms to receive us when all our friends forsake us.
1809 Finch Q: How should they serve their time?
A: With Freedom, Fervency, and Zeal.
Q: What does that point out to us?
A: Chalk, Charcoal, and Clay.
Q: Why so?
A: Because Chalk is extremely free for the use of man, which seldom touches but leaves a trace behind it; Charcoal is so very fervent, that when well lighted, few Metals can resist its force; and Clay, our mother Earth, is so eminently zealous, that she is always labouring for our good and will readily open her arms to receive us when all our friends forsake us.
1819 Hardie May every free-mason's heart have the freedom of chalk, the fervency of charcoal the zeal of friendship, but not the hardness of marble, when a distressed brother makes his demand.
1826 Morgan Q. What did you serve him with?
A. Freedom, fervency, and zeal.
Q. What do they represent?
A. Chalk, Charcoal, and Earth.
Q. Why so?
A. There is nothing freer than Chalk, the slightest touch of which leaves a trace behind; nothing more fervent than heated Charcoal, it will melt the most obdurate metals; nothing more zealous than the Earth to bring forth.
1831 Allyn W. M.: What did you serve him with?
S. W.: Freedom, fervency, and zeal.
W. M.: What do they present?
S. W.: Chalk, charcoal, and clay.
W. M.: Why so?
S. W.: There is nothing freer than chalk; the slightest touch of which leaves a trace behind; nothing more fervent than charcoal; for when properly heated, it will cause the most obdurate metals to yield; nothing more zealous than clay, or earth, to bring forth.
1860 Richardson's Monitor Master—What did you serve him with?
Senior Warden—Freedom, fervency, and zeal.
Master—What do they present?
Senior Warden—Chalk, charcoal, and clay.
Master—Why so?
Senior Warden—There is nothing freer than chalk, the slightest touch of which leaves a trace behind; nothing more fervent than charcoal, for when properly heated, it will cause the most obdurate metals to yield; nothing more zealous than clay, or earth, to bring forth.
1917 Ronayne Nothing more fervent than charcoal, to which, when well ignited, the most obdurate metals yield.
1996 Florida Official Monitor Anciently, there was nothing more fervent than Charcoal, for to it, when properly ignited, the most obdurate metals would yield
